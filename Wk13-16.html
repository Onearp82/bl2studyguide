Big Picture
What your professor is really testing

Your professor does not just want a holding. He wants you to identify the duty involved, explain why the court stopped trusting the board at the ordinary Business Judgment Rule level, name the correct test, and then tie the result back to the facts of the case. That is the whole game. A student who can say, "this is a care case," or "this is a Revlon case," and explain why, is in much better shape than a student who only remembers a slogan.

Best final-exam formula: start with BJR, identify the trigger, name the duty, apply the test, then state the holding and why that holding matters.
Court Logic
How the cases fit together
  • Van Gorkom teaches duty of care and what happens when directors approve a major transaction without enough information or process.
  • Unocal teaches enhanced scrutiny when the board defends against a threat and might be protecting itself.
  • Revlon teaches that once the company is effectively for sale, maximizing value becomes central.
  • Paramount v. Time teaches that not every premium offer triggers sale-mode duties.
  • Paramount v. QVC teaches that change of control does trigger a stronger obligation to seek the best value reasonably available.
  • Caremark teaches oversight, bad faith, and how hard it is to win when challenging board monitoring failures.
Exam Priority Stack
What to know cold
1. What duty is involved? Care, loyalty, good faith, oversight, or sale-duty.
2. What triggered a change from BJR? Poor process, conflict, takeover defense, sale of control, or monitoring failure.
3. What case controls? Van Gorkom, Unocal, Revlon, Time, QVC, or Caremark.
4. What did the court hold? Was the board protected, or did it lose protection?
5. Why does the holding matter? This is where your analysis earns points.
Week 13
Statutory Mergers and Board Process
Core doctrine
A statutory merger is not just filing paperwork. It is a board-level decision that can trigger fiduciary scrutiny. The board must approve the merger through a legally sound process, and if the process is weak, care-based problems can surface quickly.
Duty tie-in
This week connects strongly to duty of care. If the board approves a merger without enough information, without adequate valuation work, or without real deliberation, that is where Van Gorkom matters. The idea is simple, bad results do not automatically equal liability, but bad process can.
What to say on an exam
When you see a merger question, ask: Did the board inform itself? Did it deliberate? Did it understand value? Was anyone conflicted? If the board had a sloppy process, say duty of care and mention Van Gorkom as the warning case that directors cannot act like they are buying a used lawn mower off Facebook Marketplace.
Week 14
Takeover Defenses, Sale Duties, Paramount Cases, Insider Trading, Whistleblowing
Unocal
Duty / concern: loyalty-infused defensive decision-making. Trigger: the board takes defensive action against a bidder. Rule: the board must show it reasonably perceived a threat to corporate policy and effectiveness, and its response must be reasonable in relation to the threat.
Translation: if the board is fighting off a bidder, the court worries the board may be protecting itself, not just the corporation.
Revlon
Duty: sale-mode duty to maximize value. Trigger: the company is for sale, being broken up, or control is changing hands. Rule: the board's role shifts from preserving long-term corporate policy to seeking the best value reasonably available for stockholders.
Translation: once sale mode begins, strategy takes a back seat and value moves to the front seat.
Paramount v. Time
Duty question: had Revlon been triggered? Answer: no, not yet. The court allowed the board to continue pursuing a long-term strategic transaction because there was no true change of control.
Exam use: Time is your case for the idea that a board still has room to favor long-term strategy when control remains in a large public market.
Paramount v. QVC
Duty question: had sale-of-control duties been triggered? Answer: yes. Because control was shifting to a more concentrated setting, the board had stronger obligations to pursue the best value reasonably available.
Exam use: QVC is your case for saying change of control matters, and it tightens judicial scrutiny.
Insider trading and whistleblowing
These topics are less about merger duties and more about fairness, loyalty, disclosure, and enforcement. On insider trading, know the difference between the classical theory and the misappropriation theory. On whistleblowing, know that federal law can reward reporting and prohibit retaliation, especially in securities settings.
Week 15
Bankruptcy
Core structure
Chapter 7 is liquidation. Chapter 11 is reorganization. Chapter 13 is an individual repayment plan. The first practical question is always what kind of bankruptcy this is.
Two key terms
Automatic stay means most collection efforts stop at the beginning of the case. Discharge means the court determines what debts are wiped out and what debts survive. These are the two terms professors love because they separate someone who actually studied from someone who is just free-styling.
Week 16
UCC and Lawyer Soft Skills
UCC core
UCC Article 2 governs the sale of goods. Goods are movable items. Land is not a good. Services by themselves are not goods.
Memory aid: if it moves, Article 2 probably wants a word.
Why the soft-skill material matters
Your professor is making the point that legal analysis is done by people. Listening, judgment, civility, reading the room, and spotting what actually matters in the facts all affect whether a lawyer helps or hurts the client. That has exam value too, because strong exam answers are organized and practical, not robotic.

Duty-to-Case Explanations
What duty came out of each major case

Smith v. Van Gorkom

Duty of Care
Process
Merger Approval
This is the classic case for duty of care in the merger setting. The lesson is not that directors can never approve a deal quickly. The lesson is that directors cannot approve a major sale without being adequately informed. The court focused on the board's weak process, limited information, and lack of careful deliberation. Holding significance: directors can lose BJR protection when the process is grossly negligent.

Unocal Corp. v. Mesa Petroleum

Enhanced Scrutiny
Defensive Measures
Threat Analysis
Unocal is the takeover-defense case. The court understood that boards can face real threats from coercive or inadequate bids, but it also understood that directors may be tempted to defend themselves instead of the corporation. So the court created a middle-ground test. Duty significance: once the board uses defensive measures, the court stops giving automatic deference and requires proof of threat plus proportional response.

Revlon, Inc. v. MacAndrews & Forbes

Sale Mode
Value Maximization
Change in Board Role
Revlon teaches that there comes a point where the board is no longer acting as guardian of long-term corporate policy. Once the breakup or sale becomes inevitable, the board's job changes. It must focus on securing the best value reasonably available for stockholders. Duty significance: the duty shifts toward value maximization, and defensive favoritism becomes much harder to justify.

Paramount Communications v. Time

Long-Term Strategy
No Change of Control
BJR Space
Time matters because it shows the board does not always enter Revlon mode simply because a higher offer appears. The court allowed the board to continue with its strategic transaction because there was no true change of control. Duty significance: directors still had room to pursue a long-term plan under the Business Judgment framework.

Paramount Communications v. QVC Network

Change of Control
Revlon Trigger
Best Value
QVC is the companion case that shows when the board's flexibility tightens. Once control is being transferred to a more concentrated controller-type setting, stockholders are losing the future possibility of control premiums and market discipline. Duty significance: enhanced scrutiny applies, and the board must seek the best value reasonably available rather than simply prefer its favorite deal structure.

Caremark

Oversight
Good Faith
Loyalty Framework
Caremark is the oversight case. It asks whether the board failed to implement any reasonable reporting system or consciously ignored warning signs. Duty significance: this sits in the good-faith / loyalty space and is very hard for plaintiffs, because ordinary sloppiness is not enough. The plaintiff needs facts showing something closer to sustained, knowing inattention.
Presentation Case Tie-Ins
Use this wording when your professor asks, "What duty came out of that case?"
Van Gorkom, what duty and what ruling?
Duty: duty of care. Ruling: the board could be liable because it approved the merger through a grossly negligent process. Why it matters: the case teaches that directors need enough information, enough time, and enough deliberation before approving a major transaction. The court was not punishing the board for a bad outcome. It was punishing an inadequately informed decision-making process.
Unocal, what duty and what ruling?
Duty / concern: takeover-defense decisions with loyalty concerns built in. Ruling: the board may use defensive measures, but it must prove it reasonably perceived a threat and responded proportionally. Why it matters: the court does not blindly trust the board when it is fighting a bidder, because self-protection may be mixed in.
Revlon, what duty and what ruling?
Duty: sale-of-control / value-maximization duty. Ruling: once the company is for sale, the board's role changes and it must focus on getting the best value reasonably available. Why it matters: this is the turning-point case that says long-term strategy no longer carries the same force once sale mode begins.
Paramount v. Time, what duty and what ruling?
Duty question: did Revlon apply? Ruling: no, because there was no true change of control, so the board could still pursue long-term strategy. Why it matters: this case keeps students from making the lazy mistake of saying every premium bid triggers Revlon.
Paramount v. QVC, what duty and what ruling?
Duty question: did change of control trigger stronger duties? Ruling: yes. The board had to pursue the best value reasonably available under enhanced scrutiny. Why it matters: this is the case you use when control is no longer staying in a broad public market and is instead shifting into a more concentrated form.
Caremark, what duty and what ruling?
Duty: oversight / good faith within the loyalty framework. Ruling: liability can arise from an utter failure to implement reporting systems or a conscious failure to monitor them, but these claims are difficult. Why it matters: it tells you when board inattention becomes serious enough to move beyond ordinary carelessness.
Short Oral Answers
Quick, clean ways to sound like you understand the case
  • Van Gorkom: this is a duty-of-care case about an uninformed merger process.
  • Unocal: this is an enhanced-scrutiny case for takeover defenses.
  • Revlon: this is the sale-mode case, once the company is for sale the board must seek the best value reasonably available.
  • Time: this case shows the board can still pursue long-term strategy when there is no true change of control.
  • QVC: this case shows that a change of control triggers stronger value-maximization duties.
  • Caremark: this is the oversight case, and it is hard for plaintiffs because they must show extreme monitoring failure or conscious disregard.
Special Cases
Core final-exam cases, duties, and what each case teaches

Use these case panels to connect the weeks together. The exam move is to identify the duty involved, explain what factual trigger caused the court to apply a tougher framework, state the holding, and then explain why that holding matters in later cases. These cases are huge because they show how directors move from ordinary Business Judgment Rule protection into enhanced scrutiny, sale-duty analysis, or oversight liability.

Takeover and Sale Cases
Embedded summaries for the core corporate-law materials
Unocal, embedded study panel
Core facts idea: the board faced a hostile takeover setting and responded with defensive measures. The court understood that directors may honestly believe they are protecting the company, but it also understood the obvious danger that directors may really be protecting their own jobs and control.

Duty problem: this is mainly a duty of loyalty / good faith structural concern in the takeover setting. The court is not saying the board automatically acted disloyally. It is saying the conflict risk is serious enough that ordinary deference is no longer enough by itself.

Test: first, the board must show it reasonably perceived a threat to corporate policy and effectiveness. Second, it must show the defensive response was reasonable in relation to the threat posed. In class language, that means the board has to prove both the danger and the fit between the danger and the defense.

Holding significance: this case creates enhanced scrutiny for takeover defenses. It matters because it teaches that takeover law begins with distrust of easy board excuses. The board gets a chance to justify itself, but it must earn its protection back. That becomes the bridge between full BJR deference and the more demanding sale cases that follow.
Revlon, embedded study panel
Core facts idea: the corporation moved into a sale or breakup posture, and once that happened the board's mission changed. The court treated the company as being in sale mode rather than ordinary strategy mode.

Duty problem: this is where the board's ordinary freedom to choose among long-term visions narrows sharply. Once sale becomes inevitable, the board cannot hide behind broad strategic language if the real question is who is offering the best value to stockholders.

Rule: the board must seek the best value reasonably available for stockholders. That does not always mean a perfect auction is mandatory, but it does mean the board's process and preferences must be tied to value rather than favoritism, entrenchment, or soft loyalty to one bidder.

Holding significance: this case changes the board's role. It is the clearest statement that once sale mode starts, the directors become auctioneers charged with getting the best value reasonably available. On the final, this case matters because it shows the moment when preserving the corporation gives way to maximizing stockholder value.
Paramount v. Time, embedded study panel
Core facts idea: the board wanted to continue with a strategic transaction rather than immediately pivot to another attractive offer. The pressure point was whether the mere existence of a richer deal forced the board into Revlon duties.

Duty problem: did the board have to switch into sale-mode value maximization simply because stockholders could receive a better immediate price? That is the classic exam trap here.

Answer: no. The court said there was no true change of control because control would remain fluid in a large, publicly traded ownership structure. Since control stayed dispersed, the board retained room to pursue long-term corporate strategy.

Holding significance: this case protects room for long-term strategy where control stays dispersed in a public market. It matters because it teaches that Revlon is not triggered by every merger and not triggered by every higher bid. The right question is whether control is actually changing in a legally meaningful way.
Paramount v. QVC, embedded study panel
Core facts idea: the deal structure would move the company from dispersed public ownership into a more concentrated control setting. That meant stockholders were not just getting cashed out of one deal. They were also losing the future opportunity to receive a control premium later.

Duty problem: were stockholders losing the special protections and future options that exist when control remains dispersed in a public company? The court said yes, and that changed everything.

Answer: yes, so enhanced scrutiny and Revlon-style value analysis applied. Once a real change of control is on the table, the board's duties tighten because stockholders only get one shot at that premium.

Holding significance: this is the case that sharpens the difference between ordinary strategic mergers and control-shifting transactions. On the final, pair this case directly against Time. Time equals no true control shift, so more board flexibility. QVC equals real control shift, so stronger value-maximization duties and much less board freedom to play favorites.
Duty Foundations
Embedded review panels for the duty cases your professor expects you to connect
Van Gorkom, embedded study panel
Use: use this case whenever a merger or major deal was approved too quickly, with too little information, or without enough deliberation.

Duty involved: this is your flagship duty of care case. The core lesson is that directors must become adequately informed before approving a major transaction.

What the court cared about: the court did not simply say the board picked the wrong number. It focused on the board's process, speed, and lack of reliable information. That is why this case is about gross negligence in decision-making, not hindsight regret alone.

Why it matters: this case teaches that bad process can destroy BJR protection. For your final, tie Van Gorkom to Week 13 merger mechanics and explain that once a merger is on the table, the board cannot approve it casually. Major transaction plus thin process equals duty-of-care danger.
Caremark, embedded study panel
Use: use this case whenever the facts involve red flags, compliance failures, reporting-system weakness, or board silence in the face of danger.

Duty involved: it lives in the good faith / loyalty / oversight territory. Courts often describe it as one of the hardest theories for a plaintiff to win, because ordinary sloppiness is not enough.

What the plaintiff must show: either an utter failure to implement any reporting or information system, or a conscious failure to monitor or respond to the system that existed. That is much worse than a board simply missing one problem.

Why it matters: this case teaches that oversight is part of fiduciary duty. Connect it to your later weeks by remembering that directors are not only judged for deals they approve. They can also face exposure for mission-critical problems they basically stop watching.
How These Cases Connect Across the Weeks
The course map your professor is looking for
Week 13, mergers: start with Van Gorkom. It teaches that merger approval is not just paperwork. The board must be informed, deliberate, and careful. That is your duty-of-care anchor in the merger setting.

Week 14, takeover defenses: move to Unocal. Once the board starts defending against a threat, the court worries about self-protection. That is why the board has to justify both the threat and the proportionality of its response.

Week 14, sale mode: then move to Revlon. Once the company is really for sale, the board's role changes from protecting the corporate enterprise to getting the best value reasonably available for stockholders.

Week 14, the Paramount cases: Time tells you Revlon does not apply every time a higher offer appears. QVC tells you Revlon does apply when there is a real shift in control. That comparison is one of the most important final-exam connections in this part of the course.

Oversight and good faith: Caremark reminds you that directors are not judged only for takeover and merger decisions. They also have monitoring obligations. When there is a severe failure to install or use reporting systems, the duty problem becomes one of oversight, good faith, and loyalty-based responsibility.

Big takeaway: the weeks fit together as one progression. The board starts with BJR protection. Van Gorkom shows how weak process threatens duty-of-care protection. Unocal shows how defensive action triggers enhanced scrutiny. Revlon, Time, and QVC show how sale and control questions change the board's mission. Caremark shows that even outside a deal setting, directors can face fiduciary exposure when they stop monitoring serious risks.
