Big Picture
What these weeks are really about

These weeks move from the documents that structure business power, to the disclosures public companies owe the market, to rules governing worker relationships, and then back to the board of directors and stockholder litigation. In other words, the course keeps asking the same practical questions: who has authority, what must be written down, what must be disclosed, and who can sue when management goes sideways.

Week 5
Governing Documents
Articles vs. bylaws
Articles are the corporation's foundational public document. They are filed with the state and establish the entity's legal existence.

Bylaws are internal rules that usually are not filed with the state. They govern meetings, directors, officers, notice requirements, amendment procedures, and other governance mechanics.

Exam shortcut: Articles create the corporation. Bylaws run the corporation.
If the question asks where to find director procedures, stockholder meeting rules, or officer duties, think bylaws first.
Operating agreements for LLCs
Operating agreements are the LLC equivalent of bylaws, but often do even more. They can cover tax treatment, fiscal year, bank authority, ownership percentages, initial contributions, profits and losses, voting rules, management structure, conflicts of interest, fiduciary duty waivers if allowed, buy-in and exit rules, and dissolution procedures.

For LLCs, the operating agreement is where the owners try to avoid future fistfights with footnotes.
Buy-sell agreements, triggers, and valuation
Buy-sell agreements create liquidity and help control ownership transitions. Common triggers include death, disability, retirement, termination of employment, bankruptcy, divorce, deadlock, transfers to third parties, and time-based events.

Common valuation methods include book value, adjusted book value, multiple of earnings, fair market value by appraisal, and customized formulas.

A vague buy-sell provision is basically a lawsuit seed with a due date.
Week 6
Public Filings and IRAC
SEC and EDGAR basics
EDGAR is the SEC's filing system for documents submitted under federal securities laws. The SEC's mission is investor protection, fair and orderly markets, and capital formation. Public company reporting sits right in the middle of that mission.

The filing system is not random paperwork. It is the market's public record.
10-Q, 10-K, 8-K, and 14A
10-Q is the quarterly report. It includes unaudited financial statements, MD&A, material legal proceedings, controls and procedures, market risk, and risk factors.

10-K is the annual report. It includes audited financial statements and broader disclosure such as business, risk factors, legal proceedings, executive compensation material, and related transactions.

8-K reports major current events that investors should know promptly, such as leadership changes, significant transactions, material agreements, or other important developments.

14A is proxy material tied to stockholder voting, governance, board elections, and compensation matters.

Fast distinction: 10-Q is quarterly and unaudited. 10-K is annual and audited. 8-K reports current major events. 14A is the proxy world.
If the fact pattern mentions voting, executive compensation, or board elections, your antenna should go up for 14A.
Forward-looking statements
Companies often include cautionary language for projections about strategy, supply chains, demand, revenues, cost reductions, and future profitability. The point is to tell investors the future is uncertain and actual results may differ materially from projections.
IRAC example and exam writing
The Kelson / SuperEats example is less about memorizing one fake delivery driver and more about learning how your professor wants legal reasoning structured.

Issue, state the legal question clearly.
Rule, lay out the multi-factor or doctrinal standard.
Analysis, argue both sides using the strongest facts.
Conclusion, make the call and explain why.

Do not write a rule dump and call it analysis. The analysis section is where you earn your points.
Week 7
Employment and Labor Law
Employee vs. independent contractor
This distinction affects liability, benefits, taxes, wage rules, and legal protections. Courts often use a balancing test that looks at control, profit or loss from managerial skill, investment in helpers or equipment, skill, permanence, whether the work is integral to the business, the parties' label, exclusivity, method of payment, and dependence.

Labels matter some. Actual control and economic reality matter more.
Employment at will
Employment at will generally means either party can end the relationship at any time, unless a contract or statute says otherwise. But at-will does not authorize illegal discrimination, retaliation, or other unlawful grounds for termination.
Title VII, disparate treatment, and disparate impact
Disparate treatment is intentional discrimination. It also includes retaliation and pattern-and-practice theories.

Disparate impact does not require intent. A neutral policy can still violate Title VII if it has discriminatory effects and is not job related.

Intent drives disparate treatment. Discriminatory effect plus weak job-relatedness drives disparate impact.
Accommodation, defenses, and labor law basics
Title VII requires reasonable religious accommodation unless it causes undue hardship. Disability accommodation issues arise under the ADA. The ADEA protects workers age 40 and older.

Common defenses include merit, seniority, BFOQ, and some affirmative action settings. In discrimination cases, the EEOC often plays a gatekeeping role through the right-to-sue process.

Labor law adds unions, the NLRA, Taft-Hartley, and the NLRB. Section 7 protects concerted activity and union organizing, while unfair labor practices target interference with those rights.
Week 8
Board of Directors, Disney, and Stockholder Litigation
DGCL §141 and board power
DGCL §141(a) is one of the most important corporate law rules. It grants plenary power over management of the corporation to the board of directors, not to the stockholders. It also provides the statutory basis for the business judgment presumption.

Related subsections address meeting requirements, actions without a meeting, committee delegation, classified boards, reliance on experts, director compensation, and director removal.

Stockholders own the corporation, but the board manages it. That sentence pays rent on exams.
If the question asks who decides ordinary corporate business, start with the board.
The role of the board
The board functions as decision-maker, risk evaluator, overseer, and ethical checkpoint. Boards delegate a lot, but they cannot disappear into the wallpaper and pretend oversight does not exist.
Disney case
The Disney litigation is commonly used to explore fiduciary duty, board process, executive compensation decisions, and the difference between bad outcomes and actionable bad faith. The case teaches that courts care deeply about process, deliberation, and whether directors consciously disregarded their responsibilities.

A bad business result does not automatically equal a breach. Sloppy process and conscious disregard are the danger zones.
Direct vs. derivative suits
Direct suits are brought by stockholders to vindicate their own rights as stockholders. Derivative suits are brought by stockholders on behalf of the corporation to vindicate the corporation's rights.

The key distinction turns on who suffered the alleged harm and who gets the benefit of the recovery.

Ask two questions every time: Who got hurt? Who gets paid if the plaintiff wins?

Week 7
Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)
Full case analysis
Issue: Does Title VII's prohibition on discrimination because of sex include discrimination based on sexual orientation and transgender status?

Holding: Yes. Firing an employee for being gay or transgender is discrimination because of sex under Title VII.

If the employer's action turns on sex-based comparisons, Title VII is in play.
Week 7
303 Creative v. Elenis (2023)
Exam use
This case is useful here mainly as a warning sign. It is not an employment discrimination case. It deals with compelled speech and public accommodation issues, not Title VII workplace rules.

Do not stuff 303 Creative into a pure employment analysis just because the words discrimination and rights appear nearby.
Week 7
Griggs v. Duke Power Co.
Core rule
Griggs is the classic disparate impact case. A facially neutral employment practice can violate Title VII if it has discriminatory effects and is not sufficiently job related.

Think neutral rule, harmful effect, weak business necessity.
Week 8
DGCL §141
Rule and significance
DGCL §141(a) gives management authority to the board of directors and provides the statutory foundation for the business judgment presumption. Other subsections address meetings, committee delegation, expert reliance, compensation, and removal.

Default power sits with the board, not with stockholders.
Week 8
Disney Litigation
Exam takeaway
Disney is used to test fiduciary duty, board process, oversight, compensation decisions, and the boundary between bad decisions and bad faith. The case rewards students who can separate poor outcomes from legally actionable failures in board conduct.

Courts do not punish boards just because a decision looked dumb in hindsight. Process still matters.
Week 8
Tooley Test, Direct vs. Derivative
Doctrine summary
The core questions are: who suffered the alleged harm, and who would receive the benefit of the recovery or remedy?

If the corporation suffered the primary harm and the corporation gets the remedy, the suit is derivative. If the stockholder suffered the harm directly and gets the remedy directly, the suit is direct.
