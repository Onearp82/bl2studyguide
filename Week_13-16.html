Big Picture
What the final really tests

The final is not just asking what a case held. It is asking whether you can identify the board’s duty, decide which court test is triggered, and explain how the court moves from trust to scrutiny. The cleanest way to think about the whole course is this: the board starts under the Business Judgment Rule, and then specific facts knock the case into a more demanding framework like Unocal, Revlon, Caremark, loyalty review, or entire fairness.

Final shortcut: start with the Business Judgment Rule, then ask what fact breaks the presumption and what test replaces it.

Professor Emphasis
What to drill into your head
① Duties matter. Duty of care, duty of loyalty, and good faith are not floating buzzwords. They determine whether the board keeps protection or loses it.
② Triggers matter. A takeover defense does not get analyzed like a sale of control. An oversight case does not get analyzed like a merger-price case.
③ Process matters more than hindsight. Courts care about what the board knew, what it investigated, and what it ignored.
④ Math matters in a logic sense. The board starts at BJR, and each bad fact subtracts protection and adds scrutiny.
⑤ Analysis wins exams. Anyone can write the issue and the rule. The grade difference shows up in the “A” of IRAC.

Week 13
Statutory Mergers
Core idea
The statutory merger is the basic building block of M&A. The main point is not to memorize a random code section. The point is to understand that mergers are formal transactions with approval mechanics, and those mechanics sit on top of fiduciary duties.
Merger exam shortcut: board approval + shareholder approval + filing mechanics + fiduciary overlay.
What matters in practice
The board cannot just like the deal. It has to approve the deal through a legally sufficient process. That means information, deliberation, and proper procedure. Once you get into the merger setting, care, loyalty, disclosure, and price questions all start colliding.
Most testable point
Your professor is unlikely to treat mergers like pure filing trivia. He is much more likely to ask how the board should act during a merger and which duties become active. The merger setting is where the board can move from normal BJR protection into higher scrutiny fast.
Week 14
Takeover Defenses, Sale Duties, Paramount Cases, Insider Trading, Whistleblowing
Unocal
Unocal applies when the board uses defensive measures against a perceived threat. The board must show a threat to corporate policy and effectiveness and then show that its response was reasonable in relation to the threat posed.
If the board is fighting, think Unocal. If it is selling, think Revlon.
Clean test language: threat first, reasonableness second.
Revlon
Revlon applies when the corporation is effectively for sale or when control is changing hands. Once triggered, the board’s role shifts from preserving corporate strategy to maximizing stockholder value, especially in cash sale settings.
If cash auction, max money. No poetry, no vibes, just price.
Paramount v. Time
Time shows that not every attractive cash offer triggers Revlon. The board could continue its long-term strategy because the deal did not create a change of control in the sense that QVC later did. Ownership remained in a broad, public market rather than collapsing into a controller.
Paramount v. QVC
QVC shows the opposite. When a deal moves the corporation into a setting where control is effectively being transferred, the court applies enhanced scrutiny. This is where the board’s obligation to maximize value comes into focus and protective devices start looking much more dangerous.
Insider trading + whistleblowing
Insider trading connects directly to loyalty. The classical theory focuses on insiders using corporate information for their own benefit. The misappropriation theory focuses on breaching trust or confidentiality to trade, even where the information comes from another company. Whistleblowing sits on the enforcement side: it rewards reporting and protects against retaliation.
Week 15
Bankruptcy and Property
Bankruptcy core
Bankruptcy is about what happens when debt cannot be paid in the normal way. Chapter 7 is liquidation, Chapter 11 is reorganization, and Chapter 13 is an individual repayment plan over time.
Bankruptcy exam shortcut: what chapter is this, what property exists, and who gets paid first?
Automatic stay + discharge
The automatic stay hits at the start of the case and freezes most collection activity. The discharge is the endgame question, meaning what debt is wiped out and what survives.
Property
Property law here matters because bankruptcy always ends up asking what the debtor actually owns, what is real versus personal property, and what rights attach to the item. Fixtures are a classic pain point because they sit near the border of real and personal property.
Week 16
UCC and Lawyer Soft Skills
UCC core
The UCC is the practical ruleset for commercial transactions, especially the sale of goods. Article 2 governs sales of goods, which means physical movable items rather than services or land.
UCC shortcut: if it moves, Article 2 probably wants a word.
What to remember
This chapter is less about deep code memorization and more about understanding how business contracts actually function: flexibility, practical defaults, warranties, and commercial reasonableness. Lawyer soft skills matter because the law does not operate in a vacuum. Communication, listening, civility, empathy, and judgment affect whether a dispute gets solved, worsened, or needlessly litigated.
Exam use
Expect your professor to use the soft-skills material more as a practical lens than as a standalone trivia dump. It is his way of reminding you that law is done by humans, not by robots with bad social calibration.

The “Math” of Court Review
How the protection gets subtracted
Think of the board starting with 100 points of protection under the Business Judgment Rule. The court does not literally score it that way, but this makes the logic clean.
Step 1: Start with 100 = BJR presumption, board trusted.
Step 2: Add a trigger fact, like takeover defense, sale, conflict, or oversight failure.
Step 3: Each trigger subtracts trust and adds scrutiny.
Step 4: The court picks the right test and asks whether the board earns its protection back.

Examples:

  • Unocal: the board loses automatic trust because it is defending itself against a threat. If it proves threat + reasonable response, it earns protection back.
  • Revlon: the board loses flexibility because the company is effectively for sale. The math shifts from strategy protection to price maximization.
  • Caremark: the board usually keeps protection unless the plaintiff can show a truly severe monitoring failure. That is why these claims are so hard.
  • Loyalty / bad faith: this is the most dangerous subtraction. Once conflict or conscious wrongdoing shows up, the board is in real trouble fast.
Duty-by-Duty Drilldown
The duties that keep showing up in court
Duty of care
This asks whether directors informed themselves and used a defensible decision-making process. Van Gorkom is the classic warning shot here. The key phrase is not “the board chose badly.” The key phrase is “the board chose badly because it did not adequately inform itself.”
Duty of loyalty
This asks whose interests the directors were really serving. If the answer starts drifting toward themselves, a favored insider, or a controller, the case gets hotter immediately. Loyalty issues can turn what looked like a safe board decision into a major judicial problem.
Good faith
Good faith lives inside the loyalty framework. It matters when directors consciously disregard known responsibilities or behave in a way the court reads as intentional indifference. Sloppiness is one thing. Knowing indifference is another animal entirely.
Oversight / Caremark
Caremark is where the court asks whether the board had any meaningful reporting system or consciously ignored the one it had. The reason plaintiffs struggle here is that courts do not want to transform boards into perfect compliance machines. But if the board basically checks out and quits watching, the claim starts living.
Sale duties / Revlon
Revlon duties kick in when strategy gives way to sale. The board’s freedom to prefer one vision over another gets squeezed because price becomes the central legal question. That is why Time and QVC matter so much. One stayed in strategy mode. The other crossed into sale-of-control territory.
Defensive duties / Unocal
Unocal exists because courts know boards may protect the company, but they also know boards may protect themselves. So the board has to prove the threat is real and the response is proportionate. No lazy fear. No overkill. That is the middle lane Delaware built.
Fast Trigger Guide
When to say which test
Question says board fought off bidder → say Unocal
Question says company is effectively being sold or control shifts → say Revlon
Question says board ignored compliance, safety, or monitoring → say Caremark
Question says conflicted directors or self-dealing → say loyalty / entire fairness danger
Question just says board made a normal decision and there is no bad fact yet → start with BJR
IRAC Simplified
The easiest way to stay organized under pressure
I = Issue → what legal question is actually being asked?
R = Rule → what doctrine or test governs?
A = Analysis → apply the facts to each part of the rule and explain why the court would care.
C = Conclusion → likely outcome.
Most students can do I and R. The grade gap is in A.
The “A” Part — How to Actually Analyze
Use this every single time
Step 1: Identify the trigger. Why is this not just a normal BJR question anymore?
Step 2: Name the correct test. Unocal, Revlon, Caremark, loyalty, or ordinary care?
Step 3: Break the test into parts. Do not analyze the doctrine as one blob.
Step 4: Attach one or two facts to each part. Courts care about facts attached to elements, not your feelings about fairness.
Step 5: Explain what side each fact helps. This is where you sound like a lawyer rather than a person shouting from the bleachers.
Step 6: Make the mini-conclusion before the final conclusion. Example: “Because the board investigated the threat but adopted a very aggressive defense, prong one is likely satisfied but prong two is contestable.”
IRAC Templates You Can Reuse
Plug-and-play analysis language
Unocal template
Issue: Whether the board’s defensive response to the bid should be upheld.

Rule: Under Unocal, the board must show a threat to corporate policy and effectiveness and must show that the defensive response was reasonable in relation to the threat posed.

Analysis: The board will argue there was a real threat because ________. That helps satisfy prong one. However, the challenger will argue the response was too extreme because ________. That makes prong two the key fight. If the court thinks the defense was proportionate, the board likely earns protection back. If not, the court will scrutinize the board much more closely.

Conclusion: The outcome turns on whether the response was proportionate.
Revlon template
Issue: Whether the board had Revlon duties and, if so, whether it satisfied them.

Rule: Once the corporation is for sale or control is shifting, the board must focus on maximizing stockholder value.

Analysis: Revlon is likely triggered because ________. Once that happened, the board could no longer justify its decision mainly on long-term corporate vision. Instead, the court would ask whether the board pursued the best value reasonably available. If the board favored one bidder for reasons unrelated to value, that weighs against it. If it ran a fair process and had a defensible price-based reason, that helps the board.

Conclusion: The key is whether the board stayed loyal to value maximization after Revlon was triggered.
Caremark template
Issue: Whether the board breached its oversight obligations.

Rule: Under Caremark, a plaintiff generally must show either an utter failure to implement a reporting or information system or a conscious failure to monitor the system that existed.

Analysis: The plaintiff will argue the board failed because ________. If the facts show the board had no reporting structure at all, that supports the first Caremark path. If the board had a system but ignored repeated warnings, that supports the second path. The board will respond that it had systems in place and was not consciously indifferent. Because Caremark claims are so hard, the plaintiff needs facts showing something closer to sustained inattention than ordinary imperfection.

Conclusion: The claim is strongest if the board basically stopped trying to monitor a mission-critical risk.
Why Analysis Feels Hard, and How to Fix It
The cure for blank-page panic
Analysis feels hard because students try to write the whole answer at once. Do not do that. Instead, write in chunks: rule part one, fact, effect. Rule part two, fact, effect. That is it. You are not writing a movie script. You are connecting dots. Once you stop trying to sound brilliant and start trying to sound structured, the analysis starts writing itself.
Best shortcut: every sentence in analysis should answer, “why does that fact matter under this rule?”
