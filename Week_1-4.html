Big Picture
What is agency law?

Agency is the cornerstone of all organized business activity. Because corporations can only act through people, nearly every commercial transaction involves an agent. Agency law determines when principals are bound by agents' contracts, when they bear liability for agents' torts, and what duties each side owes the other.

Chapter 38
Agency Relationships — Formation & Duties
Creating an agency relationship
An agent acts in the name of and on behalf of a principal, with authority to affect the principal's legal rights and obligations toward third parties.

Three elements required:
1. Consent — both parties agree to the relationship
2. Control — principal has the right to control the agent's conduct
3. On behalf of — agent acts for the principal's benefit

No formal contract or payment is required. Agency can arise informally through conduct and behavior — the parties cannot privately label someone an "independent contractor" if the actual relationship is that of employment.
Two ways to create an agency:
— By agreement (express, oral or written)
— By operation of law (implied by conduct or emergency circumstances)
Five types of agents
General Agent — broad, ongoing authority; can conduct a wide range of business on the principal's behalf. To restrict authority, the principal must spell out limitations explicitly. A general power of attorney is a common personal general agent relationship.

Special Agent — authority limited to one specific task or transaction only. Example: a real estate broker hired only to find a buyer cannot also sign the purchase contract on behalf of the seller.

Agency Coupled with an Interest — agent has a property interest in the subject matter (e.g., a literary agent who earns a % of all royalties). The principal cannot unilaterally terminate this agency to the agent's detriment.

Subagent — appointed by the agent (not directly by the principal). If the general agent had authority to hire subagents, both the principal and the general agent may be liable for the subagent's conduct.

Servant / Employee — principal controls not just the result but also the method and manner of work. Creates the greatest liability for the principal under respondeat superior.

Independent Contractor — principal controls only the result, not how work is done. Principal generally NOT liable for IC's torts.

Key distinction: If the principal controls HOW work is performed → employee. If the principal controls only the final result → independent contractor. Courts look at actual conduct, not labels in contracts.
Exam factors for IC vs. employee: Does the employer set hours? Reimburse expenses? Withhold taxes? Is pay salary or commission? Does the worker use their own tools? Was the worker hired for a specific project or ongoing work?
Agent's duties to the principal (fiduciary)
The agent owes a fiduciary duty — the highest standard of loyalty and care.

Duty of Loyalty — must not act for personal gain at the expense of the principal. Self-dealing and secret profits are strictly prohibited. Example: John Alden wooing Priscilla for himself while serving as Captain Standish's agent.

Duty of Care — must perform tasks competently and responsibly.

Duty of Confidentiality — cannot disclose or use confidential information, even after the agency ends.

Duty to Obey — must follow the principal's lawful instructions.

Duty to Notify — must inform the principal of all material facts. Notice to the agent = notice to the principal.

Breach of fiduciary duty → agent must surrender all profits and may be liable for damages to the principal.
Principal's duties to the agent
Duty to Compensate — pay the agreed fee or reasonable value if not specified.

Duty to Reimburse — cover expenses and losses incurred while following principal's instructions.

Duty to Indemnify — protect the agent from losses caused by the principal's own conduct.

Duty to Cooperate — not interfere with the agent's ability to perform the assigned tasks.
Chapter 39 — Authority
Four Types of Authority (Critical for Exams)
A. Express authority
Directly granted by the principal, orally or in writing. The strongest form of authority. Third parties may fully rely on written authorizations (e.g., signature cards at a bank).

The principal bears the risk if the agent misuses expressly granted authority. Example: Allen Funt gave his accountant express authority via signature cards; when the accountant embezzled, the bank was not liable — Funt bore the risk of the authority he granted.
B. Implied authority
Authority to perform acts reasonably necessary to carry out express authority. Arises from the nature of the relationship, industry customs, and the agent's knowledge of the situation.

A business manager has broad implied authority: hiring/firing employees, paying debts, selling inventory, taking in receipts.

Limitation: No implied authority for unusual or extraordinary actions — cannot sell part of the business, take on debt, start a new business, or move premises without explicit permission. Example: A hotel manager named Andy has implied authority to reorder stationery but NOT to rename the hotel.
Emergency exception: If unforeseen circumstances make it impossible to contact the principal and inaction would cause substantial loss, the agent may take necessary extraordinary actions. (G.H. Mumm Champagne case — importer filed suit when France was under German occupation.)
C. Apparent authority (highly tested)
Exists when a third party reasonably believes the agent has authority based on the principal's conduct or representations — not the agent's own claims.

Flows from: principal → third party (not from principal to agent).

Based on estoppel: the principal cannot deny authority when the third party detrimentally relied on the principal's representations.

Can arise from prior dealings — if a supplier dealt with a business under one owner, and the business is sold, the old owner is still liable to the supplier until the supplier receives actual written notice.

O'Banner rule: Apparent authority requires actual, detrimental reliance by the third party — not just the appearance of authority. If no reliance is proven, the principal is not bound.
Exam checklist: (1) Did the PRINCIPAL create the appearance of authority? (2) Did the third party ACTUALLY and REASONABLY rely on it? (3) Was the reliance detrimental? All three must be true.
D. Ratification
When a principal approves an unauthorized act after the fact, retroactively making it binding as if it had been authorized from the start.

Requirements:
1. Principal must have knowledge of all material facts
2. Principal must accept or affirm the entire transaction (cannot pick and choose)
3. The agent must have been purporting to act on the principal's behalf at the time of the act

Once ratified, the contract is treated as binding from the beginning. The principal cannot later deny it.
Chapter 39 — Tort Liability
Respondeat Superior & Scope of Employment
Direct vs. vicarious liability
Direct liability — principal is personally at fault: instructed the agent to commit the act, or was negligent in hiring/supervising an unfit agent.

Vicarious liability (respondeat superior) — "let the master answer." The employer is liable for an employee's torts committed within the scope of employment, even without the employer's personal fault.
Detour vs. frolic
Within scope → employer is liable.

Detour — a minor, brief deviation from job duties. Employer is still liable. Example: delivery driver makes a quick stop for lunch during a route.

Frolic — a major deviation entirely for the employee's personal purposes, unconnected to work. Employer is NOT liable. Example: employee leaves work entirely for hours to handle personal business and causes an accident.

Courts weigh: time elapsed, distance traveled, nature of the deviation, and whether the employee had re-entered the scope of work before the tort occurred.
Exam tip: The line is fact-specific. A small personal stop during a work trip = detour (still liable). Going across town for personal hours = likely frolic (not liable). Re-entry into scope also matters — if the employee was heading back to work when the accident happened, some courts find the employer liable again.
Independent contractor rule and exceptions
General rule: a principal is not liable for the torts of an independent contractor.

Exceptions (principal IS liable):
1. Non-delegable duties — duties the law requires the principal to personally ensure (e.g., safe premises for invitees)
2. Inherently dangerous activities — blasting, demolition, handling hazardous materials
3. Negligent selection — principal hired an incompetent IC while knowing of the risk
Corporate Law
Corporate Powers, Purpose & Veil Piercing
Corporate powers (DGCL §121–122)
Under the Delaware General Corporation Law — the most influential corporate statute in the U.S.:

§121 — General Powers: Corporations have all powers necessary and convenient to conduct any lawful business.

§122 — Specific Powers include: Make contracts, own and transfer property, sue and be sued, borrow money, issue stock, adopt and amend bylaws, make charitable contributions.

Corporations derive powers from state statute, not inherent rights. Any act beyond the corporation's granted powers is called "ultra vires."
Corporate purpose — shareholders vs. stakeholders
Shareholder Wealth Maximization — the traditional view: directors must maximize shareholder value as their primary obligation (Dodge v. Ford; eBay v. Newmark).

Stakeholder Theory — a growing view: corporations should also consider employees, communities, customers, and the environment. Some states (benefit corporations) explicitly allow this.

eBay v. Newmark rule: Directors of a for-profit corporation must act in the best interests of shareholders. Explicitly subordinating shareholder value to social goals is a breach of fiduciary duty — even if the social goals are admirable.
Piercing the corporate veil
Normally, shareholders have limited liability — only their investment is at risk. The corporate veil shields them from personal liability for the company's debts.

Courts will pierce the veil and hold owners personally liable when two elements are proven:

1. Unity of interest — the owner and corporation are not truly separate (failure to observe formalities, commingling of funds, the owner treats the company as their personal piggy bank)

2. Inequitable result — treating them as separate would sanction fraud or fundamental unfairness

Walkovszky v. Carlton: Multiple taxi shell corporations, each minimally capitalized. Court REFUSED to pierce. Undercapitalization alone is not enough — must also show fraud or inequity.
Kinney Shoe v. Polan: Corporation had no assets, no bank account, never held meetings. Court PIERCED. When the corporation is a pure facade — an alter ego — the veil will fall.
Exam checklist for piercing: (1) Failure to observe corporate formalities? (2) Commingling of personal and corporate funds? (3) Severe undercapitalization? (4) Use of the corporation to perpetrate fraud? More factors = more likely to pierce.

Apparent Authority
O'Banner v. McDonald's Corp.
Full case analysis
Issue: Was McDonald's Corp. liable under apparent authority for the actions of a franchisee?

Holding: No. McDonald's was not liable.

Reasoning: Even if the plaintiff believed the franchisee was a McDonald's employee, apparent authority based on estoppel requires proof that the plaintiff actually and detrimentally relied on that belief. The plaintiff provided no such evidence — she would have entered the store regardless of who owned it.

Rule: Apparent authority is based on estoppel. Estoppel requires detrimental reliance. Without proven reliance, there is no apparent authority and the franchisor is not bound.

The consumer's general impression that a franchise location = the parent company is NOT sufficient. The plaintiff must prove she specifically relied on the belief of agency in a way that harmed her.
Corporate Purpose
eBay Domestic Holdings Inc. v. Newmark
Full case analysis
Background: Craigslist's founders (the Newmarks) adopted a poison pill and other defensive measures against eBay, explicitly stating they wanted to run Craigslist as a public service rather than maximize profits for shareholders.

Issue: Can directors of a for-profit corporation formally subordinate shareholder value to social mission goals?

Holding: No. The directors breached their fiduciary duties to shareholders.

Rule: Delaware law requires directors to act in the best interests of shareholders. While directors may make conservative business decisions and consider stakeholders incidentally, they cannot make avoiding profit a matter of explicit corporate policy.

Key distinction: The business judgment rule protects conservative or cautious management decisions. But explicitly declaring the company won't be run for profit is a breach — not protected by the BJR.
Veil Piercing — Refused
Walkovszky v. Carlton
Full case analysis
Facts: Carlton owned 10 taxi corporations, each owning 2 cabs and carrying only the minimum required insurance. A pedestrian was severely injured and sought to hold Carlton personally liable by aggregating all the corporations.

Holding: Court refused to pierce the veil. Carlton was not personally liable.

Reasoning: The corporations, while thinly capitalized, observed corporate formalities. No fraud was alleged. The structure, though minimally funded, was a recognized industry practice.

Rule: Undercapitalization alone is not enough to pierce the corporate veil. The plaintiff must also show fraud, injustice, or that the corporation was operated as a personal alter ego.

However: The court left open enterprise liability — if plaintiff could show all the corporations were operated as a single enterprise (not separate entities), liability could potentially reach all of them.
Veil Piercing — Granted
Kinney Shoe Corp. v. Polan
Full case analysis
Facts: Polan formed Industrial Realty Co. to sublease from Kinney Shoe's subsidiary. Industrial had no assets, no bank account, never issued stock, and never held a corporate meeting. Polan contributed no capital to it.

Holding: Court pierced the corporate veil. Polan was personally liable.

Reasoning: Industrial was a complete alter ego — it had absolutely no independent corporate existence. It was purely a liability shield with no substance behind it.

Rule: When the corporation is a mere instrumentality of the owner and treating them as separate would sanction fundamental injustice, courts will pierce.

Contrast with Walkovszky: Carlton had real (if minimal) corporate operations. Polan's corporation had literally nothing. The degree of disregard for the corporate form is the key variable.
Express Authority
Allen A. Funt Productions v. Chemical Bank
Full case analysis
Facts: Allen Funt gave his accountant express authority (via signature cards on file at the bank) to draw checks on the company account. The accountant used this authority to embezzle funds. Funt sued the bank for negligence in failing to catch the scheme.

Holding: Bank was not liable. Suit dismissed.

Rule: A third party (the bank) is fully entitled to rely on express authorization given by a principal to an agent. The principal bears the risk when the agent misuses expressly granted authority. The principal cannot shift that risk to a third party who reasonably relied on the authorization.

Takeaway: Express authority is a double-edged sword. You get the benefit of authorized agent acts — but you also bear the risk if the agent misuses that authority.
