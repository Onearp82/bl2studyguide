Big Picture
What these weeks are really about

These weeks build a complete fiduciary-duty framework. Week 10 establishes the protective shell around directors through the Business Judgment Rule and exculpation. Week 11 shows when that protection cracks because the board was uninformed or conflicted. Week 12 pushes into good faith, oversight, and mergers, where the board’s role becomes both more demanding and more exposed. Across all of it, the course keeps returning to the same practical questions: was the board informed, was it loyal, did it actually monitor, and who bears the risk when process breaks down?

Week-by-Week Flow
The sequence your professor is building
Week 10
BJR + care
Week 11
Van Gorkom + loyalty
Week 12

That sequence matters. It moves from protection, to process failure, to oversight and transaction structure. It is basically teaching when directors are safe and when they are in the blast radius.

Class Emphasis
What your professor keeps signaling
① Process matters more than outcome, courts generally do not punish directors just because a result went badly.
② Loyalty problems are worse than care problems, because conflict and bad faith are more dangerous than mere sloppiness.
③ Oversight claims are brutal to win, but a board cannot disappear into the wallpaper and pretend monitoring is optional.
④ Mergers are where doctrine gets expensive, because care, disclosure, loyalty, and approval mechanics collide.
⑤ Risk allocation is everywhere, whether the topic is a boardroom decision or a contract draft.
Exam Priorities
What is most testable
① BJR as a presumption, and who carries the burden to rebut it.
② Van Gorkom as the classic uninformed-board case, where bad process drives liability.
③ Duty of loyalty versus duty of care, especially conflict, self-dealing, and bad faith distinctions.
④ Good faith inside loyalty, not as some random free-floating extra duty.
⑤ Caremark’s two-prong oversight framework, plus why the claim is so hard to plead.
⑥ Statutory merger mechanics, especially board approval, shareholder approval, and filings.
Week 10
Duty of Care, BJR, and 102(b)(7)
Business Judgment Rule
The BJR is a presumption that directors acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action taken was in the company’s best interests. That means courts start by protecting directors, not by attacking them.
Exam shortcut: directors get the shield first, plaintiffs have to break it.
If the question asks who has the burden early, the challenger usually does.
How BJR gets rebutted
The classic ways to rebut the presumption are conflict of interest, illegality, fraud, bad faith, and gross negligence. A merely disappointing result is not enough.
A bad result is not the same thing as a bad process.
102(b)(7) and exculpation
A charter provision under Delaware law can eliminate or limit personal liability for money damages for duty of care violations. It does not eliminate exposure for loyalty breaches, bad faith, intentional misconduct, or knowing violations of law.
Care-only plus money damages can be a dead lawsuit fast.
Why this week matters
This week sets the baseline. Directors are not insurers. Corporate law is built to encourage decision-making, not to punish every swing and miss.
The law wants effort, information, and honesty, not hindsight perfection.
Week 11
Smith v. Van Gorkom, Shlensky, and Duty of Loyalty
Smith v. Van Gorkom
Van Gorkom is the classic case where directors lose because the board approved a major merger decision too quickly and without being adequately informed. The board did not really do the homework needed for a decision of that size.
A deal can look attractive and still be legally defective if the board process was careless.
Use the phrase gross negligence and uninformed decision-making.
Shlensky as the contrast case
Shlensky shows the opposite instinct. The board decided not to install lights at Wrigley Field and the court refused to second-guess the decision because it was a board judgment without obvious conflict or disqualifying process failure.
Shlensky is deference. Van Gorkom is process collapse.
Duty of loyalty
Duty of loyalty focuses on whether directors are acting for the corporation or for themselves. Self-dealing, conflict, and bad faith move the analysis into much harsher territory than ordinary care cases.
Care is about process. Loyalty is about motive, conflict, and whose interests are being served.
Why this week matters
This is where you learn that the shield from Week 10 is not absolute. If directors are uninformed or conflicted, courts start looking much harder and the risk rises fast.
This is the week where the shield starts to crack.
Week 12
Good Faith, Caremark Oversight, and Statutory Mergers
Duty of good faith
Good faith is generally treated as part of the duty of loyalty framework. It is violated when directors consciously disregard responsibilities or knowingly tolerate serious legal problems.
Negligence is sloppy. Conscious disregard is dangerous.
Caremark oversight liability
Caremark and later Stone v. Ritter frame oversight liability through two major paths: directors utterly fail to implement any reporting or information system, or directors implement one and then consciously fail to monitor it. These claims are famously difficult to win.
Caremark is one of the hardest theories in corporation law for a plaintiff to win on.
If the board tried to monitor and built systems, the plaintiff is usually climbing uphill in boots filled with concrete.
Mission-critical oversight
Modern cases sharpen the doctrine by asking whether the ignored issue was mission critical to the company’s business. If a board ignores its most essential risk area, the plaintiff has a much stronger shot at surviving dismissal.
If the company lives or dies on one category of risk, the board better be watching it.
Statutory mergers
A statutory merger normally requires board approval, shareholder approval, and filing with the state. These transactions bring together fiduciary duty, disclosure, process, and approval mechanics in one setting.
Mergers are where doctrine stops being abstract and starts costing real money.

Week 10
Aronson / Business Judgment Rule
Core significance
This is where the classic BJR presumption language comes from. It frames the idea that courts assume directors acted properly unless the challenger pleads enough to rebut that assumption.
Presumption first. Attack second.
Week 10
Malpiede v. Townson
Exam use
Malpiede matters because if a corporation has a 102(b)(7) provision and the plaintiff pleads only a duty of care claim seeking only money damages, the case can be dismissed because there may be no remedy left.
No remedy, no party.
Week 11
Smith v. Van Gorkom
Full case analysis
Issue: Did the directors breach their duty of care by approving a merger without being adequately informed?

Holding: Yes. The process was too thin for a decision of that magnitude.

Van Gorkom is the case you cite when the board jumped before it looked.
Week 11
Shlensky v. Wrigley
Core rule
Shlensky reminds you that courts are not supposed to run baseball teams, or corporations for that matter. If a non-conflicted board makes a business choice, the court usually backs off.
Judicial humility is part of corporate law too.
Week 11
Duty of Loyalty
Doctrinal summary
Loyalty cases focus on whether directors are serving the corporation or serving themselves, a favored insider, or some interest that cannot be squared with fiduciary obligation.
If conflict shows up, your danger meter should spike.
Week 12
Caremark / Stone v. Ritter
Oversight framework
The two prongs are the backbone of oversight liability: no reporting system at all, or a conscious failure to monitor the system that exists. Stone later clarifies that this sits in the loyalty / good-faith world.
No system or no monitoring, those are the two doors in.
Week 12
In re Boeing
Modern exam takeaway
Boeing matters because it shows modern courts taking mission-critical oversight more seriously when the board appears to have ignored the company’s central operational risk.
If airplane safety is mission critical, the board should probably talk about airplane safety. Wild idea, I know.
Week 12
Statutory Merger Doctrine
Why this matters
This is the transaction setting where all the fiduciary concepts become practical. Board approval, shareholder approval, and filings are formal steps, but behind them sit care, disclosure, loyalty, and price questions.
Procedure is not filler. It is the skeleton of the transaction.
